Company Manifesto

Why ownership matters in the age of intelligence.

Frontier Intelligence Capital is built around a structural bet: as artificial intelligence makes more forms of knowledge work scalable, owning the productive assets that capture the resulting surplus becomes increasingly important — if that surplus is captured rather than competed away or redistributed. This manifesto explains why we believe it will be, and what would tell us we are wrong.

Core motivation

AI raises productivity. Companies use AI to automate work, reduce labor intensity, and increase output per employee. The surplus created by that productivity does not distribute evenly: a significant portion accrues to margins, platforms, infrastructure providers, and shareholders — when the providers retain pricing power, when competition does not arbitrage the gains to customers, and when regulation does not redistribute them. These conditions are not guaranteed. They are our central bet.

1. AI progress is becoming economically visible

AI is no longer only a research promise or a better autocomplete. Frontier systems can reason across long context, use tools, inspect files, write code, analyze documents, generate media, summarize research, answer customers, and coordinate multi-step workflows.

Software engineering is the clearest early signal. AI can now generate code, inspect repositories, debug errors, write tests, and execute implementation tasks that previously required scarce human capacity. The same pattern is spreading into research, finance, operations, legal review, marketing, support, sales, and management workflows.

The signal in financial statements is equally visible: hyperscaler capex is still accelerating, semiconductor margins remain at structural highs, and the largest software platforms have explicitly framed AI as a margin-expansion mechanism rather than a top-line story. We treat this as evidence, not narrative.

2. Companies have a rational incentive to automate

Firms do not adopt AI because it is fashionable. They adopt AI because it can reduce cost, increase speed, improve consistency, and raise output per employee. In competitive markets, work that can be converted into software, agents, or repeatable AI workflows will face automation pressure.

AI does not merely help workers produce more. It helps companies need fewer workers for the same unit of output.

The 2023–2025 pattern of layoffs combined with rising revenue-per-employee at large technology companies is not anomalous; it is the leading edge of a longer arc.

3. Productivity gains can flow through ownership channels — under specific conditions

This is the central claim of the thesis, and the place where we must be most careful.

When AI reduces labor intensity, the financial gains can be captured through higher margins, stronger platform economics, greater demand for compute, more valuable data and distribution networks, and higher returns to shareholders when cash flow is converted into reinvestment, buybacks, or durable growth. But this capture is conditional. It requires:

  • Pricing power against substitution. AI providers must maintain enough differentiation that open-source models, alternative architectures, or new entrants do not commoditize their core offering before reinvestment compounds it into a moat.
  • Competition that does not arbitrage the gains away. In some industries, productivity gains historically flow to consumers in the form of lower prices — electricity, computing, telecom long-distance, retail. Whether AI-driven gains stick to producers or transfer to customers depends on industry structure and switching costs.
  • Regulatory and fiscal stability. Windfall taxes, antitrust action, AI-specific regulation, and labor-side redistribution policies could meaningfully reduce the share of surplus that reaches equity holders.
  • A weak form of Jevons. If productivity gains are aggressively reinvested into new product surface, new geographies, and expanded headcount, total labor demand can rise even as per-task automation increases — shifting gains from margin expansion back toward operating cost.

We believe these conditions hold in aggregate for the large US technology complex over a multi-year horizon. We do not believe they are self-evident. This is a bet on a configuration, not an observation of a law.

4. What would tell us we are wrong

A thesis that cannot be falsified is not a thesis. The following would constitute material evidence against the ownership-capture frame:

  • Hyperscaler gross margins compressing for two or more consecutive years while AI capex remains elevated — suggesting capex is not converting to durable economic profit.
  • Open-source model capability closing the gap with frontier proprietary models faster than inference cost falls — suggesting commoditization is winning.
  • Sustained decline in revenue-per-employee at AI-leveraged platforms while headcount stabilizes — suggesting Jevons-type reinvestment is dominating margin expansion.
  • Material regulatory action — AI-specific windfall taxes, mandated open weights, or aggressive antitrust against the platform layer — that systematically transfers surplus away from shareholders.

We monitor these signals deliberately. The point of writing them down is to commit, in advance, to recognizing the regime change rather than rationalizing it after the fact.

5. The investment response

Given the bet, and given the conditions, the practical response is not to chase every AI application. It is to own the productive public-market assets most likely to compound from the intelligence economy — semiconductors, cloud platforms, software infrastructure, distribution networks, and companies with pricing power and operating leverage — while sizing positions and managing leverage as if any of the conditions above could break.

Frontier Intelligence Capital manages proprietary capital only. The framework is long-term, public-market focused, ownership-oriented, and explicitly risk-aware. We are not betting that AI will work; that question is largely settled. We are betting on a specific distribution of who captures the surplus, and we hold that bet with the awareness that the distribution can shift.

This is what separates the approach from thematic AI funds, mechanical leveraged buy-and-hold systems, and discretionary tech momentum: the unit of conviction is not "AI is good" but "ownership of the surplus-capturing layer is the right exposure, contingent on the conditions in Section 3, monitored against the signals in Section 4."

Core principles

  1. Ownership over dependency. As more work becomes scalable, productive assets become a more durable participation layer.
  2. Public markets as access layer. Many important AI beneficiaries are already public companies.
  3. Compounding with risk control. Exposure matters, but only if duration, liquidity, drawdown, and concentration are managed.
  4. Quality over novelty. Scaled incumbents with distribution, data, pricing power, and capital discipline are the most likely surplus-capturers.
  5. Conditional conviction. Strong views, weakly held on falsifiable conditions. Strategy must be explainable, repeatable, and resilient under stress — including the stress of being wrong.
